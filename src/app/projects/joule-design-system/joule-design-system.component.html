Building a Design System for Joule

In early 2017 my team was tasked with migrating a widely used cooking app to a new framework. I decided to use this opportunity to establish a scalable design system.

Up until that point, the app had been built relatively piecemeal, and the experience reflected this. We frequently heard complaints from users about things like inconsistent navigation patterns and the limited visibility of core features. In addition, engineers often didn’t have bitesize components to work with and were even duplicating work across different features.

Problems

  • There was an opportunity to greatly improve the design/development workflow
  • Design was lacking a clear source of truth
  • Important knowledge was frequently undocumented
  • UX patterns needed to be consolidated
  • There was a general need to move more quickly

In order to address these issues, I began by trying to understand the scope of the problem. I spoke with each engineer working on the app to better understand their workflows and did a comprehensive design audit of the existing app.

Finding a Solution: Sketch + Github

I decided to utilize a combination of a Sketch symbol library and Github to make a system that would allow designers to collaborate more efficiently while also introducing version control. I also wanted to include the design specs in an environment that our engineers were already comfortable with. Forcing design to take on a similar workflow as our engineering team would more closely integrate the teams and make it easier for our engineers to start utilizing our specs as a part of their daily process.

Typography

Before the design system, our app didn’t have a consistent style guide for type. While we had two main brand fonts, sizes and weights varied across views and even prominent headlines weren’t synced. Therefore, I created a clear type ramp with rules for when specific styles could be used and eliminated a lot of the variance.

Note: While the rest of the design system was built on an 8px grid, I intentionally decided to leave type off of this grid in order to give us more flexibility with type sizes.

Color

While the app did include some commonly used colors, similar to type, it also had a ton of variance. I decided to greatly reduce this complexity by pulling things back to 6 primary hues, and 6 gradations of gray/black. The goal was to eventually do a full color study and add multiple shades for each primary hue.

Iconography

Since we were introducing an 8px grid, I rebuilt many of our icons to fit the grid system and additionally added dozens of new icons and illustrations. Icons were organized into 2 general buckets: 1) Smaller icons for the majority of complex views 2) Larger, more illustrative icons for less UI heavy views.

Components

Because I was working from an existing product, my first step was to do an audit of all the existing components and patterns. I went through every view in the app and identified opportunities to both consolidate existing patterns and improve on components.

Next I made sure to think about components holistically, especially with regards to how they would nest within each other. Because my entire system was based on a grid, this ended up working relatively well.

For example, a simple “list-item” component was built with “insert slots” that could take any kind of sub-component: icons, buttons, text, photos, and inputs. This system allowed us to have a lot of flexibility in assembling our UI while leveraging the maximum amount of shareability.

Leveraging the power of Sketch

Sketch’s symbols gave us a lot of flexibility when sharing files across the team. Because I could customize which overrides were acceptable for each symbol, I was able to reduce the amount of slack in the system.

I also made sure that there was parity with the naming of the variables/overrides across the github specs and sketch symbols so that designers and engineers were discussing components with the same language.

Using Github As a Source of Truth

Making Github an essential part of the design workflow ended up working really well. Once we had a component’s functionality and visual elements finalized, we’d submit a PR that included our sketch files, changes to the sketch library, and any written documentation and specs for that component. This allowed engineers to reference these PR’s when changes to a component were made.

Beyond component specs, we also started including all of our project specs in github to keep all of our work in one place: links to research docs, goals, logging, prototypes, etc.

Not only did this make sharing knowledge within Design straightforward, it also empowered engineers to dig deeper into the thinking behind the design.

Learnings

Maintaining design specs is hard. Components are always evolving, and the whole system is on the edge of being out of date. It takes a lot of discipline to revisit early specs after changes have been made in production.

Don’t let your tools constrain your thinking. While building a component system can greatly increase your efficiency, it can also sometimes act as a blocker to creativity. On different occasions I’d find myself approaching a problem from the perspective of using our existing system rather than letting myself freely explore unique solutions.

Your engineering team is also one of your users. Don’t underestimate the power of thinking empathetically about the struggles of your teammates and how you can make their jobs easier. It’s amazing the ways it will boost collaboration, generate new ideas, and speed up the process.
