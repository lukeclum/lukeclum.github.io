Joule Sous Vide Cooking

What I learned redesigning a sous vide cooking experience.

Overview

In the fall of 2016 we (ChefSteps) released Joule, a connected sous vide device, to the world. But a year and a half later, the core cooking UX needed some substantial improvements.

As the first fully connected sous vide device, Joule is controlled by a companion app that allows users to set Joule to specific temperature, monitor their cook, and guide users through a comprehensive cooking process. Sous vide, for those that don’t know, is a precise cooking method that uses water to help cooks achieve very predictable results. TLDR1: Joule makes incredible food and is very flexible to use.

However, due to initial hardware constraints, Joule does not have physical controls and therefore pushes all of the its functionality to its app. With frequent connection issues, multi device support, multiple sources of truth, error cases, and other distributed systems problems, the app needed a robust and dependable solution. TLDR2: The system is complicated but the app can’t suck.

Identifying the problems

In late 2017, I was asked to lead the design efforts for Joule. To better understand the current state of the app, I began by running a qualitative user study where I interviewed 15+ Joule users to better understand their cooking habits, pain points, and joys of using Joule. It was a fast, overwhelming flood of data that helped bring to light a few common themes. Next we did an analysis of all our reviews from Amazon, Apple, and Google.

Within the core cooking UX, we discovered a few problems:

  • Users were often missing notifications and didn’t have any information to help them recover
  • There was confusion around when they should add food
  • There was confusion around the differences between our “manual” and “guided” cook modes.
  • It took a few cooks for users to have a comprehensive overview of the process of sous vide cooking.
  • Often times users didn’t know what their connection status was which exacerbated a few already troubling connection issues.
  • From a business perspective, we needed a much more flexible UI that could scale with new features.

Goals

Following these studies, we decided that we needed to take a fresh look at our cooking UX. To guide our design process, we decided to focus on:

  • Providing users with a lot more data about their cook to help with planning and/or recovering from an error state.
  • Simplify and front load touchpoints where possible
  • Reduce and consolidate UX patterns
  • Build in as much flexibility into the UI as possible

After a few rounds of design, prototyping, and usability testing with both current and perspective Joule users, we landed on our next iteration.

A more comprehensive cooking dashboard

Simple, Glanceable Status

To help cut through the chaos of the kitchen, we designed a simple, glanceable, element that would quickly communicate the status of the cook, and showcase only the most relevant information.

Detailed timelines and cooking stats

To help with missed notifications and meal planning we included a comprehensive timeline of cooking events that allow users to have detailed information about their current cook.

Autostart Timers

Arguably the most important feature of the redesign, we decided to encourage users to drop their food early and let Joule automatically start their timer once their water was heated. However, due to a few considerable technical issues, we rolled this into its own separate project. Click here to learn more about the design process.

Perfect Before

One of the major benefits of sous vide is the fact that you don’t necessarily have to remove your food as soon as it’s complete. Instead, it’s flexible enough to be ready when you are (For example, you can leave your steaks in the water until your friends arrive). In order to communicate this value and help users feel confident about letting their food continue to cook, we released a new feature that let users know how long they could leave their food before the results would start to change.

Better Error States

Error states were redesigned to follow a consistent pattern that helps users troubleshoot the problem and recover when possible.

Simple and Familiar Recipes

We simplified the recipe layout to follow more common usability patterns and be much more scannable.
